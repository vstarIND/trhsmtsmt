When Abusing Parents Becomes “Cool”: A Classroom Reality No One Talks About

By The Rising Hindustan • February 2026

There is a silent culture spreading through teenage classrooms — one that rarely appears in official reports, parent-teacher meetings, or school magazines. Yet students see it every day.

It is the normalization of abusing someone’s parents in the name of humor.

A mother’s name shouted across the room. A father’s name twisted into an insult. Laughter follows. Claps follow. Approval follows.

And just like that, humiliation becomes entertainment.

How It Begins

It rarely starts with hatred. It starts with imitation. One influential student says something bold. Others laugh. The attention feels powerful.

Soon, more students copy the pattern. Because in teenage peer culture: Shock value equals popularity. Volume equals dominance. Disrespect equals fearlessness.

The one who insults the loudest is often seen as the strongest. And the one who reacts emotionally becomes the weakest.

The Victim’s Reality

But what about the student whose parents’ names are being used?

He forces a smile. He laughs with them to avoid isolation. He pretends it doesn’t matter.

But inside, something shifts.

Parents represent security, sacrifice, and identity. When their names are used casually as abuse, it doesn’t just hurt pride. It attacks belonging.

The damage is subtle at first: Confidence drops slightly. Participation reduces slightly. Trust in classmates weakens slightly.

But repetition turns slight damage into silent erosion.

The Psychology of Superiority

Why do some students rely on parental insults to feel superior?

Often, it is not strength — it is insecurity. When academic pressure is high and comparison is constant, some students search for alternative ways to dominate socially.

Insulting someone’s parents becomes a shortcut. A performance of power. A way to control the emotional space of the room.

The louder the humiliation, the higher the social reward.

And when bystanders laugh instead of objecting, the culture becomes normalized.

The Emotional Consequences No One Records

Schools measure marks. They record attendance. They track discipline.

But they do not measure humiliation.

They do not record how many times a student went home replaying insults in his mind. They do not record how many times someone sat quietly instead of answering a question because their confidence was shaken.

Some students begin isolating themselves. Some develop bottled anger. Some lose focus academically.

And in extreme cases — when repeated humiliation combines with academic pressure, comparison, and fear of retaliation — vulnerable students may begin having dangerous thoughts. Not because they are weak. But because they feel trapped.

When Silence Becomes Complicity

The most dangerous part of this culture is not the insult itself. It is the silence around it.

When classmates laugh. When no one intervenes. When teachers overlook “just joking.”

That silence sends a message: This behavior is acceptable.

And once disrespect becomes acceptable, empathy begins to disappear.

A Classroom Should Build Character

Education is not only about grades. It is about values.

A classroom should be a place where confidence grows — not where dignity is tested daily.

Real superiority is not humiliating someone’s parents. Real strength is self-control.
